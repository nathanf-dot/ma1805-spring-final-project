The Sound of Direction is an experimental interactive maze game that challenges the player to navigate without relying on vision. Instead of visual guidance, the player must depend on audio cues, spatial awareness, and memory to find the exit. The idea was to simulate blindness and was partly inspired by the Marvel superhero Daredevil. My original concept didn’t have much direction, but as I began developing the project, I started to understand what it could become. Alongside the themes of sensory limitation and perception, I leaned further into a sonar-based aesthetic, which is reflected in the pulse mechanic that briefly reveals the environment. I found it particularly engaging learning how to program the sound system that guides the player, especially experimenting with frequency and how subtle changes in pitch can communicate distance and direction.

The project explores themes of sensory substitution and digital perception, specifically how users interpret and navigate space when a dominant sense is removed. By stripping away visual clarity, the experience forces the player to rely on sound and memory, creating moments of uncertainty, frustration, and gradual understanding. The reset mechanic, where the player is returned to the start after hitting a wall, reinforces this idea by encouraging careful movement and learning through repetition rather than trial-and-error alone. There is also an underlying interest in how game systems can shape behaviour, particularly how audio feedback can become intuitive over time.

Technically, the project was built using p5.js, with a grid-based maze system controlling movement and collision. The core interaction is driven by an oscillator-based sound system, where pitch is mapped to the player’s distance from the exit, creating a “hot and cold” navigation method. In addition to this, a melodic path system rewards correct movement with structured musical notes, subtly guiding the player along the intended route. Implementing these systems required an understanding of coordinate mapping, real-time input handling, and basic sound synthesis, all of which were new areas that developed throughout the project.If i had more time, I would work on having some sort of instructions before the start of the game to inform the player of the movement mechanics (W,A,S,D) and the sonar pulse trigger (SPACE KEY). 

The project was influenced by a range of sources. The concept of navigating without sight was inspired by Daredevil, particularly his use of heightened senses to interpret space. Visually and conceptually, the sonar aesthetic draws from submarine radar interfaces, which informed the pulsing visual language and minimal interface design. Additionally, experimental games and media that prioritise sound over visuals, such as Blindscape, helped shape my understanding of how audio can function as a primary navigational tool rather than a secondary feature.
